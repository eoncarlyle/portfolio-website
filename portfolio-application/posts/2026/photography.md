# Photography

My first proper digital camera was a Canon Rebel T1i. It was the type of camera you'd buy a photography-curious highschooler for Christmas if it was 2010, but I got my hands on one almost a decade later in 2019. The model was discontinued for a while by then, but my Rebel was a Craigslist find my one of my sisters that she never got enough use from to justify keeping. When I was home one weekend in May that year she asked if I would take it off her hands, and I left with my first DSLR.

Strangely for how old I am the Rebel wasn't my first camera. That was my Canon AE-1, a camera from the late 70s and early 80s that shoots film. It was a birthday gift from my other sister while I was still in high school a few years before, this was when the film photography resurgence was getting started. This was the camera that taught me the basics of photography. I shot many blurry or underexposed photos as I was getting the grips with how shutter speed, aperture, and film speed impacted a photo. Luckily there was an on-camera light meter which made this easier: when partially pressing down on the shutter button the viewfinder shows the most appropriate aperture. According to my dad this made shooting film a lot easier: before the days of onboard light meters you'd need to consult paper charts to get the same information.

To be honest the 'against the grain' nature of film photography was part of the appeal. This of course confused many adults in my life who lived through the inconveniences of film photography - carrying film, paying for each photo, or losing undeveloped rolls due to mechanical mishaps - and didn't understand why anyone would willingly subject themselves to that when point-and-shoot cameras were getting better and cheaper every year. To be honest, I started to understand it once I started using the Rebel T1i.

From January to August in 2019 I worked as a co-op Chemical Engineer in training in Appleton, Wisconsin. A few people in the office were pretty good photographers and taught me a thing or two about my new-to-me DSLR, and one of them gave me a good deal on my first fixed focal-length lens, a 50mm that I used for several years. At 80,000 people Appleton isn't a small town, but I knew few people outside of work and had a _lot_ of time on my hands, so I'd walk by the river with both the Rebel and the AE-1 and take many mediocre photos on my path to taking less mediocre photos.

I returned to Minneapolis that fall to finish my final three semesters at Minnesota. I didn't find much time to shoot during that first semester back, but much of that had to do with being back on campus with my then girlfriend (now wife) and freinds. The next time I thought about either of my cameras was when classes moved to Zoom after spring break in March of 2020. We didn't know it yet, but despite being over a year out from graduation I already had my final in-person college class. This was far from the most important supply chain crunch in the early pandemic, but the global webcam supply was not exactly sufficient for the literal millions more people who needed on for remote class or work that spring. Some Reddit posts explained how to make older Canon DSLRs work as a webcam. The drivers could be a little flaky and I sometimes had to reconnect, but it worked well enough and became part of my remote class setup:

Unfortunately for my wallet, I was hooked. Even once you could get a decent webcam in 2021, I had gotten too used to having adjustable camera settings and a narrow depth-of-field. But the somewhat flaky webcam drivers were getting old, so I upgraded to a Canon M50 Mk II which could more reliably be used as a webcam through an HDMI capture card. This was the camera that I did all of my video job interviews on to close out senior year. To this day I occasionally get a 'are you a streamer?' or 'do you have a podcast?' when on a video call with someone new.

Given that I'm probably not out shooting photos if I'm on a video call and vice versa it didn't really make sense to have a second camera, but "camera as webcam" was starting to win out over "camera as actual camera" just because breaking down and setting back up my workspace every time I wanted to shoot photos introduced just enough friction that I didn't take the camera out enough. Any time I'd do an international trip I'd make sure to bring it and I always shot enough to make me glad I brought it, but there were enough times that I regretted not bringing my camera that this year I bought a tripod head for my desk that connected to a quick-release plate at the bottom of the camera. I loosen one dial, remove the HDMI cord, and replace the AC powered dummy battery with a real one to shoot. When I come back it takes me 15 seconds to undo the process and my camera is right where I want it for video calls.

I wouldn't say I'm a great photographer in that I don't edit my photos, my white balance is on automatic, and I shoot JPEG rather than uncompressed RAW files which are better for editing. A real photographer could probably teach me a thing or two if they went through my camera settings. I've taken more photos since my wife and I's honeymoon last year, and it has made me think about taking a class or doing other structured learning. But a decent handle on the basics can take you pretty far. Understanding the 'exposure triangle' relationship between shutter speed, aperture, and film speed as well as the rule of thirds and some intuition about visual balance of photographs is enough knowledge to take better photos after only an hour or two. I'd rather invest time in getting more out of my existing camera and lenses than investing in better equipment; I seldom feel that my gear is holding me back. My camera has a smaller cropped sensor than what you'd see in a flagship mirrorless or DSLR camera and I still use the kit lens from the Rebel T1i, but I'd have to have a good reason to upgrade to a better camera body.

Most people in technical disciplines should have a creative outlet of some form, and photography is just generally fun. It is a great complement to walking and a great travel activity: exploring a new place on foot is fun, but it is nice to have something to show for it and a way to jog your memory about a past trip. I made sure to bring my camera on a Memorial Day trip to the Upper Peninsula of Michigan, where I took the picture below. If it isn't my favourite it sure is close, but I wasn't even looking through my camera when I took it. The rocks below were maybe 200 feet down the cliff I was standing on, and the cliff's edge looked a little sketchy so I held the camera out over the ledge and blindly pressed on the shutter.
